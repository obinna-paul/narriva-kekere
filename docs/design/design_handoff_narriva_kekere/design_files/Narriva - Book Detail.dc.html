About the book

When old Bashorun dies, he leaves his daughter Tinuke two things: a failing salt business on the Badagry coast, and a leather ledger he kept hidden for forty years. The business she understands. The ledger she does not — until she realises every name in it belongs to someone her father informed on, sold out, or buried.

As colonial-era debts surface in a modern city that would rather forget them, Tinuke must decide what a daughter owes the dead, and what she owes the living she's only now learning to name. The Salt Roads of Badagry is a novel about inheritance — of money, of guilt, and of the long, salt-scarred roads that carried a family to where it stands.

Adaeze Okonkwo writes the Lagos coast the way it actually smells: brine, diesel, and the particular sweetness of a secret about to keep.

About the author

Adaeze Okonkwo

Adaeze Okonkwo writes literary fiction about Lagos's coastal underworld. Born in Badagry and raised between the salt flats and the city, she spent a decade as a journalist before turning to fiction. The Salt Roads of Badagry is her first novel.

One — The Ledger

They buried her father on a Tuesday, in the kind of heat that made the gravediggers stop twice for water, and Tinuke understood for the first time that grief had a smell. It was salt. It was always going to be salt.

The Bashorun salt works had been failing for years — everyone in Badagry knew it, the way everyone knows the things a family will not say aloud — but the man himself had never failed at anything in the open. He paid his debts on the day they were due. He greeted his enemies by name. When he died, three of those enemies came to the funeral and wept, and Tinuke watched them and thought: you are not crying for him. You are crying because now there is no one left who knows.

She found the ledger that night, in the false bottom of the drawer where he kept the good kola. It was bound in leather gone soft as skin, and the first page held a single line in her father's careful hand, dated forty years before she was born.

She read the name written there. Then she read the second name. By the third, her hands had begun to shake, and she had to set the book down on the table and stand at the window where the sea air could reach her, because the fourth name in her father's ledger of the betrayed was her mother's, and the fifth, written in fresher ink, was her own.
