Why your first chapter is probably your third

Tobi Williams
Editorial Director · 6 min read
featured photo · landscape

Almost every manuscript that lands on my desk opens in the wrong place. Not badly — often the prose is lovely, the scene is competent, the writer clearly worked hard. The problem is that the real book hasn't started yet. It starts on page eleven, or in chapter three, after the author has finished clearing their throat.

This is not a flaw. It's how writing works. You need those early pages to find your way in — to remember who these people are and what they sound like. The mistake is leaving them in the finished book, where the reader has no such need.

The opening you agonised over is usually scaffolding. Good scaffolding — but you take scaffolding down before anyone moves in.

Here's the test I use. Find the first moment in your manuscript where something is genuinely at stake — where a character wants something and can't simply have it. Mark it. Then read everything before it and ask, honestly, whether the reader would miss it if it were gone. Most of the time, they wouldn't. Most of the time, that mark is your real first line.

It feels like loss, cutting those pages. It isn't. The information in them — who these people are, where we are, what world we're in — almost always resurfaces naturally once the story is actually moving, and it lands harder when it's woven through action instead of front-loaded as setup.

Start where the trouble starts. The rest is throat-clearing, and your reader has come to hear you sing.
